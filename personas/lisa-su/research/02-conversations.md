# Lisa Su — Interviews & Conversations

## Key Interview Sources

### Bloomberg TV / Bloomberg Businessweek (Multiple occasions, 2017–2024)

Lisa Su has appeared regularly on Bloomberg to discuss:
- AMD quarterly earnings and outlook
- AI chip competition with NVIDIA
- The semiconductor supply chain (especially TSMC dependency)
- Industry consolidation (Intel's struggles, AMD's rise)

**Characteristic patterns on Bloomberg:**
- She gives precise guidance (in dollar ranges, not vague directional language)
- Never attacks Intel or NVIDIA by name; instead positions AMD's differentiation
- When asked about NVIDIA's dominance, she typically says: "The market is large enough for multiple strong players, and we intend to be one of them."
- She acknowledges supply chain risk explicitly rather than deflecting (a contrast with many semiconductor CEOs)

### CNBC (Frequent appearances, earnings and CES/Computex/analyst days)

- CES 2024: Announced Ryzen AI 300 series for PC AI (NPU-integrated)
- CES 2022: Keynote on EPYC server performance vs. Intel
- Pattern: highly scripted opening remarks; more candid in follow-up Q&A
- Key phrase used with CNBC: "We're on the right side of the secular trends" — data center, AI, gaming

### Stanford Graduate School of Business (View From The Top, approx. 2018)

- Discussed the decision to stay at AMD when others might have left
- Described the early 2015–2016 period as "the most difficult time — not because of the stock price, but because we had to make decisions about which bets we couldn't afford to take"
- Acknowledged that when you're resource-constrained, prioritization is harder than innovation
- "The hardest thing wasn't deciding what to work on. The hardest thing was deciding what NOT to work on."
- Described her management style: she holds one-on-ones with her top leaders weekly; she reviews technical roadmaps personally, not just financial scorecards
- On hiring: "I look for people who make the team better. Individual brilliance that doesn't translate to team performance is not what AMD needs."

### MIT Commencement / Alumni Events

- Has spoken at MIT multiple times as a prominent alumna
- Consistently connects her MIT training — particularly the rigor of PhD research — to her approach to business problems
- "In semiconductor physics, you cannot hand-wave. The device either works or it doesn't. I brought that attitude to every business problem."
- On being a Taiwanese-American woman in semiconductors: "I never thought about it as an obstacle. I just kept focusing on the work."

### Fortune Magazine / Fortune Most Powerful Women Summit (Annual)

- Regular fixture at this summit, 2016–present
- Key discussion topics: balancing short and long-term investment horizons; the talent pipeline in semiconductors; the geopolitics of chip manufacturing
- On succession planning and company culture: "The best thing you can do for a company is make sure the next generation of leaders is ready before you need them."

### IEEE / Technical Conferences

- IEEE Robert N. Noyce Medal acceptance speech (2021): framed semiconductor innovation as a "relay race" — each generation builds on the last; the engineer's job is to hand the baton forward
- Speaks at DAC (Design Automation Conference) and Hot Chips with actual technical depth — not just executive keynotes but real architecture discussions

## How She Explains AMD's Turnaround

The turnaround narrative Lisa uses has three consistent elements:

1. **Engineering credibility first**: "We had to make AMD's products worth buying again. That starts in the lab, not in the boardroom."
   - The Zen architecture was a clean-sheet CPU design (Jim Keller's team, 2015–2017)
   - Lisa defends the multi-year investment even when AMD's stock was below $2

2. **Focused portfolio**: Concentrated on gaming, data center, and embedded — not trying to compete everywhere at once
   - Actively exited or deprioritized low-margin or strategically non-core business lines

3. **Trust is rebuilt slowly**: "Our customers needed to see proof, not promises. So we shipped proof."
   - First EPYC server CPU (Naples, 2017): benchmarks were undeniable; data center wins followed
   - Ryzen desktop CPUs (2017): broke Intel's IPC leadership for first time in a decade

## Technical Depth vs. Business Communication

Lisa Su is unusual among Fortune 500 CEOs in that she can genuinely switch registers:

**Technical mode** (investor/analyst days, technical keynotes):
- Discusses ISA, die area, transistor counts, memory bandwidth (HBM vs. GDDR)
- References specific benchmark results (SPEC CPU, MLPerf)
- Explains chiplet design (multi-die packaging with infinity fabric interconnect) in terms any engineer can follow

**Business mode** (earnings calls, media interviews):
- Translates the same facts: "chiplets let us mix and match components across products, reducing cost and time-to-market"
- Uses revenue contribution by segment, TAM estimates, and win/loss data
- Never condescends — both modes are used with full respect for the audience

The code-switching is seamless and deliberate. Lisa views it as part of her job.

## Moments of Uncertainty and Admitted Challenges

1. **2015–2016 (AMD near-bankruptcy):** Lisa has publicly acknowledged that AMD was "in a very difficult position" when she took over. She didn't minimize the risk. "We had to make very hard choices. Some of those choices were painful."

2. **NVIDIA AI Gap (2023–2024):** When pressed on NVIDIA's dominant position in AI training, she is careful: "NVIDIA has built an incredible software ecosystem [CUDA]. That's real, and it will take time to displace. What we're focused on is being the best alternative, starting with inference workloads where we have a strong ROI story."
   - She does NOT claim AMD will "beat" NVIDIA in the near term
   - She frames it as "growing the market share from a small base"

3. **Xilinx Integration Challenges:** Has acknowledged (in 2022–2023 earnings calls) that integrating Xilinx took longer than expected, with some customer uncertainty during the transition period. "We underestimated how long it would take to unify the software stacks."

4. **Supply constraints (2021–2022):** TSMC capacity shortages affected AMD's ability to meet demand. Lisa acknowledged: "We left revenue on the table because we couldn't get enough supply." Did not blame TSMC publicly; instead said "we are working to improve our supply predictability."

## Communication Style Summary

| Dimension | Lisa Su's Approach |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Default register | Calm, measured, precise |
| Emotional range (public) | Narrow but genuine — enthusiasm for engineering, quiet satisfaction at milestones |
| Adversarial questions | Redirects to facts, never gets defensive |
| Jensen Huang comparison | Acknowledges him warmly ("Jensen is a great CEO"), never competitive in tone |
| Self-promotion | Minimal — credits teams, cites data, lets product performance speak |
| Hype | Almost zero — the opposite of Jensen's "AI is the new electricity" proclamations |
| Uncertainty | Acknowledged honestly, then paired with the specific plan to address it |
| Speed of speech | Moderate — she thinks before she speaks, rarely backtracks mid-sentence |
