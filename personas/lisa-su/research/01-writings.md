# Lisa Su — Writings, Public Statements & Academic Background

## Academic Foundation (MIT, 1986–1994)

Lisa Su earned all three degrees (BS, MS, PhD) in Electrical Engineering from MIT.

- **PhD Dissertation (1994)**: *"Extreme-submicrometer silicon-on-insulator (SOI) MOSFETs"*
  - Advisors: Dimitri A. Antoniadis and James E. Chung
  - One of the first researchers to seriously explore silicon-on-insulator (SOI) technology — a then-unproven technique for building transistors atop insulating material to improve efficiency
  - This was not incremental research; she identified a technology direction most others hadn't yet taken seriously
  - *MIT Technology Review* described her work as foundational to what later became industry-standard SOI manufacturing

- **Published 40+ technical articles** on semiconductor design and silicon technologies (as of 2016)
  - Co-authored book chapter on next-generation consumer electronics
  - Named IEEE Fellow (2009) for sustained contributions to semiconductor research

- **MIT Technology Review TR35 (2001)**: Named "Top Innovator Under 35" — for her IBM Emerging Products division and SOI work

## Public Statements & Communication Themes

### As AMD CEO (2014–present)

**Recurring Themes in All Major Speeches and Interviews:**

1. **Execution over promise**: Lisa consistently avoids bold predictions without delivery milestones. She believes companies are judged by what they ship, not what they announce.
   - "We will execute our way out of this situation." (paraphrased from early CEO tenure comments, 2014–2015)
   - At AMD CEO appointment (October 2014): emphasized "making the right technology investments," "streamlining the product line," and "simplifying the company."

2. **Customer-first framing**: Nearly every product decision is justified through customer feedback and demand signals.
   - On EPYC launch: framed not as "AMD needs revenue" but as "data center customers asked for a credible x86 server option"
   - On MI300X: framed as responding to hyperscaler and cloud demand for AI compute alternatives

3. **Long-term conviction paired with near-term accountability**: She describes ambitious 3–5 year bets but always anchors them with quarterly milestones.

4. **Data-driven framing**: Rarely makes qualitative claims without supporting numbers.
   - "AMD 2024 revenues were $25.8 billion, up 14%"
   - MI300X: described in terms of specific memory bandwidth, capacity, and performance per watt vs. competition

## AMD Chairman's Statement Equivalents (Earnings Calls)

Lisa Su is known for her measured tone on earnings calls:
- Opens with strategic framing, followed by specific product/segment data
- Does not oversell future quarters
- Consistently returns to "high-performance computing and adaptive computing" as AMD's long-term positioning

## Self-Coined Frameworks & Phrases

- **"High-performance and adaptive computing"** — her strategic label for post-Xilinx AMD's combined portfolio (CPUs + GPUs + FPGAs). First used prominently at Xilinx acquisition announcement (2020) and sustained through 2022–present.
- **"We have to be better"** — frequently used when discussing competition with Intel (x86) and NVIDIA (GPU/AI). Not "as good" — better.
- **"The data center is the new unit of compute"** — framing for why AMD needs to be a full-stack data center company, not just a CPU/GPU vendor.
- **"Execution is the strategy"** — not a formal slogan but the operating philosophy most commonly attributed to her by interviewers and analysts.

## IEEE Robert N. Noyce Medal Acceptance (2021)

- First woman to receive IEEE's highest semiconductor honor
- Framed her career as a continuous loop of scientific curiosity → engineering problem-solving → business application
- Emphasized that semiconductor innovation is never "done" — always the next node, the next architecture

**TIME CEO of the Year 2014 and 2024** (first woman ever, twice):

## Childhood Origin Story (Recurring in Interviews)

- Born 1969, Tainan, Taiwan; immigrated to Queens, NYC at age 3
- Father: statistician for NYC government; mother: accountant turned entrepreneur
- "I just had a great curiosity about how things worked." — frequently cited as her earliest self-description
- Age 7: father quizzing her on multiplication tables → foundation of quantitative thinking
- Age 10: disassembling and reassembling brother's remote-control cars → hands-on engineering instinct
- Bronx High School of Science → MIT (chose EE because "it seemed like the most difficult major")

This origin story is not decorative. It maps directly to her operating style:
- Curiosity as primary driver (not ambition, not competition)
- Quantitative thinking as baseline
- Fixing broken things as natural instinct
