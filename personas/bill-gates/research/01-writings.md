# Bill Gates — Writings Research

## GatesNotes Blog (gatesnotes.com)

Bill Gates has maintained his personal blog since around 2010. Core recurring themes:

### Annual Year-in-Review
- Each December, publishes reflections on the past year and outlook for the coming year
- Always data-driven: cites progress on polio eradication, malaria deaths prevented, children vaccinated
- Signature frame: "The world is getting better, but not fast enough"
- Typically ends with 5 recommended books — the annual reading list has become a cultural event

### Annual Book Lists
- 5+ books per year, spanning history, science, economics, fiction
- Frequently recommends: Steven Pinker, Vaclav Smil, Hans Rosling (Factfulness), economic development texts
- Reads ~50 books per year; takes notes in the margins
- The book list signals what *problems* he's currently thinking about — not random curation

### Technology Essays (GatesNotes)
- "The Age of AI Has Begun" (March 2023) — called AI "as fundamental as the creation of the microprocessor, the personal computer, the Internet, and the mobile phone"
- Climate essays: supports nuclear energy (TerraPower), direct air capture, clean cement/steel
- COVID pandemic writing: championed mRNA vaccines and COVAX; warned about pandemic preparedness gaps as early as 2015 TED talk

### Recurring Themes Across All Writing
1. **Software as leverage**: The belief that code, once written, can be infinitely replicated — this is why software companies scale in ways physical businesses cannot
2. **Systems thinking**: Diseases, poverty, education — all addressed as interconnected systems with feedback loops, not isolated problems
3. **Technology for equity**: Vaccines, diagnostic tools, agricultural innovation — technology specifically targeted at those with least access
4. **Measurement matters**: Obsessive use of metrics — DALYs (disability-adjusted life years), child mortality rates, net enrollment ratios — as tools for prioritizing interventions
5. **Impatient optimism**: The world is improving by data; the pace of improvement is the problem

## Books Authored

### The Road Ahead (1995)
- Co-authored with Nathan Myhrvold and Peter Rinearson
- Published November 1995, #1 bestseller
- First edition underestimated the internet's importance — Gates famously revised the paperback edition (1996) to dramatically upgrade his internet predictions
- Self-correction on the internet is a key data point: he was wrong, he admitted it, he pivoted
- Key predictions that came true: friction-free capitalism, information highway, digital wallets, personalized media
- Key prediction missed: internet as a competitor to Windows (he framed the internet as complementary)

### Business @ the Speed of Thought (1999)
- Argued that digital nervous systems (networked information infrastructure) would separate winners from losers in business
- Central thesis: "Velocity of information flow determines competitive advantage"
- Written at the height of Microsoft's dominance and the antitrust battle
- Prophetic about enterprise digitization; silent about the rise of open-source and web platforms

### Source Code: My Beginnings (2025)
- First of three planned memoirs (second covers Microsoft years, third philanthropy)
- Covers childhood through late 1970s founding of Microsoft
- Reviewers noted unusual self-deprecation for a tech titan
- The Guardian: "There is a sense of the writer, older and wiser, trying to redeem the past through understanding it better"
- WSJ: "unexpectedly revealing account"
- Gates described it as wanting to understand how he became who he is

## Gates Foundation Annual Letters

Started with the William H. Gates Foundation, then the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation from 2000. Since 2009, published as formal Annual Letters.

### Key Letters and Themes
- **2009**: "We Can Eradicate Polio" — set a measurable, seemingly impossible goal
- **2014**: "Three Myths That Block Progress for the Poor" (with Melinda) — refuted pessimism about development aid with data
- **2015**: "Our Big Bet for the Future" — predictions for the next 15 years: mobile banking, vaccines, agricultural productivity for the poor
- **2018**: "Warren and Bill" — guest-written by Warren Buffett; their friendship as the foundation of the Giving Pledge
- **2022**: Post-divorce; Bill wrote alone; emphasized foundation's independence from personal circumstances

### Structural Patterns in Foundation Letters
- Open with a story (a specific child, a specific village, a specific disease)
- Pivot to data (here's what the numbers show)
- Acknowledge failure (here's what didn't work)
- Propose a system-level fix (here's what needs to change structurally)
- End with call to action or conditional optimism

## Open Letter to Hobbyists (1976)

This early writing is foundational to understanding Gates:
- Written when Microsoft BASIC was being pirated widely
- Argued that software developers deserve payment like hardware manufacturers
- "Who can afford to do professional work for nothing?"
- Deeply unpopular with the open-source/hacker ethic of the time
- Positioned Gates as believing in intellectual property as the engine of software progress
- The letter pre-figures every subsequent Microsoft licensing strategy
- Tension: the man who wrote this letter later gave away $50+ billion

## "Tidal Wave" Internet Memo (1995)

Internal Microsoft memo, later published:
- Gates declared the internet "the most important single development" since the IBM PC
- Redirected entire Microsoft product strategy toward internet
- Demonstrates systems thinking: one memo changed hundreds of product roadmaps
- Also reveals competitive anxiety: he saw Netscape as an existential threat

## Writing Style
- Dense with data and statistics
- Self-referential to his own reading (frequent citations of books he's read)
- Uses "I think" as genuine uncertainty marker, not false modesty
- Willing to revisit past positions and revise (The Road Ahead paperback; acknowledging mobile miss)
- Mathematical precision: "10 million children die every year of preventable diseases" (not "millions")
- Rare use of emotional language in professional writing; prefers empirical framing
